I: Installing Paco

Complete the following steps to install Paco on your Android phone.

Step 1) Prerequisites: To install Paco, you must have

a) An Android phone running OS 2.2 or better.

b) A Gmail account. This is the same account you gave to be added to the beta server. (Note, for those who have Google Apps Domains, these accounts will not work. It needs to be a normal Gmail account.)

b1) First time setup of your gmail account on your phone:

You may have already configured your gmail account on your phone previously. If not, follow these steps.

Step 1: Launch the "Settings" application.

Step 2: Choose "Accounts & sync".

Step 3: If there is not already an account for the whitelisted gmail address under Manage accounts, click the "Add account" button and follow the steps to create a Gmail account.

BUG NOTE: We don’t yet support choosing from multiple gmail accounts on the phone. If you have multiple gmail accounts on your Android phone, it will pick one automatically. Be sure to create your experiments on the server with that same gmail account or publish to your other account, otherwise, you won’t be able to see your experiments.

Step 2) Enable Sideloading of Applications: Paco has not been released yet, so it is not yet available on the market for download. Most phones allow the user to load applications that are not available on the Android Market but it requires a settings change.

For Android 3 or less,

  1. Open the ‘Settings’ application.
  2. Choose ‘Applications’.
  3. Make sure that the ‘Unknown Sources’ checkbox is checked.

For Android 4 Users:

  1. Open the ‘Settings’ application.
  2. Choose ‘Security’
  3. Make sure that the ‘Unknown Sources’ checkbox is checked.

Step 3) Install Paco

  1. Go to "Get Android" in the menu bar above.

II: Finding & Joining an Experiment

Complete the following steps to join an experiment on your Android phone.

Step 1) Prerequisites: To join a user study, Paco has to be installed (see the instruction above).

Step 2) Find an experiment

  1. Either, follow these 3 steps:
  1. Step 1: Open Paco
  2. Step 2: Click on “Find Experiments”. You might need to press ‘Refresh Experiments’ if there are none showing.
  3. Step 3: Click on an experiment to learn about it. You can see who a description, who created it, and what the general schedule is.
  1. Or, scan the barcode of an experiment sent to you
  1. If you need to install the barcode scanner:
  1. Go the the Market, click on search, and type in barcode scanner. Install the app, called ‘Barcode Scanner’, by ZXing.
  1. Once the barcode is scanned, select “Open Browser”. This will open Paco to the experiment.

Step 3) Join an experiment

When looking at an experiment you like on your phone, press “Join this Experiment”.

  1. On the page titled “Data handling”, read through the agreement and check “I consent” if you consent to the terms that the experimenter has described.
  2. On the next page, press “Save Schedule”. You can change the schedule if necessary to make it appropriate for your normal experience.

Refreshing an Experiment that has changed

Method 1

When you click ‘Refresh Experiments’ in the ‘Find Experiments’ screen, this causes all definitions to be updated, including experiments already joined.

Method 2

In the ‘Running Experiments’ screen, click on the Experiment Title you want to refresh.

This will open the experiment input screen. Press the menu button on the phone.

This will bring up a menu. Choose ‘Refresh Experiment’ to refresh the experiment.

When it is updated, you will be taken out of the experiment and back to the main screen.

III: Participating in an Experiment

Now that you have joined an experiment, depending on the schedule, it will either signal you at certain times, at random times, or, you will just self-report answers when appropriate according to the instructions for the experiment.

Signalled Response

In the signalled response, you will receive a notification in the tray at the top of the screen that says ‘Experiment Name Time to Participate’  accompanied by an alert sound and/or a vibration, depending on how you have your phone alert you to incoming messages. Make sure it is loud enough for you to hear.

Step 1: When you receive one of these notifications, press on it in the notification tray. This will open the experiment form.

Step 2: Fill out the form as appropriate.

Step 3: Press ‘Save Response’ to submit your response. You will then see a confirmation page.

Step 4: You may hit the ‘Back’ Arrow on your phone at that point to leave the screen.

NOTE: Don’t worry if you backed out without hitting save. If you go back into Paco and refill out the form, it will associate it with the intended signal until it either times out or you have saved a response in time.

Self Report Response

You may fill out the server at your own discretion as well. If there is still an active notification for a signal, it will be recorded as a response to that signal.

Step 1: Open Paco

Step 2: Click on “Running Experiments”.

Step 3: Click on ‘Experiment Name.’

Step 4: Fill out the form as appropriate.

Step 5: Press ‘Save Response’ to submit your response. You will then see a confirmation page.

Step 6: Press the ‘Back’ arrow on your phone to get back to the main screen of Paco.

Step 7: Press the ‘Back’ arrow on your phone again to leave Paco.

IV: Leaving an Experiment

At some point an experiment may be over, or you may need to leave for whatever reason. You may either stop the experiment, or uninstall Paco altogether if you do not want to participate in other experiments.

Stopping an Experiment

Step 1: Open Paco

Step 2: Click on “Running Experiments”.

Step 3: Click on the Stop Sign icon next to the experiment you want to stop. This will remove the experiment from your Running Experiments list and will stop signals to participate. You will still be able to see your data here on the server.  and in the new feature, ‘Explore Data’ on your phone.

Uninstalling Paco Completely (Why would you want to do this :-) ?)

Step 1: Open the ‘Settings’ application.

Step 2: Click on “Applications”

Step 3: Click on “Manage Applications”

Step 3: Click on “Paco”.

Step 4: Click on ‘Uninstall’.

V: Creating & Editing Your Own Experiment(s)

The real power of Paco is being able to create your own experiments easily and quickly. Currently, you create experiments on the website. However, as soon as you save the experiment definition, it is available to be downloaded to your phone.

Creating an Experiment

Note: This first time, or if you reset your cookies, you will need to login with your gmail account credentials. Paco won’t be able to see any of your account data, it just uses Google’s account authentication system to prove who you are. The Google login screen will ask if you want to give Paco access to your account for login purposes. Say ‘yes’ for Google to let Paco know that your credentials are valid. Say ‘no’ if you are uncomfortable with this. Feel free to email us at paco-users@googlegroups.com with questions.

Step 1: Click on “Administer Experiments” in the menu above.

Step 2: There will be a popup with two options. Click on “Create New”.

Step 3: This will take you to the Experiment Definition Page where you can fill in the details of your experiment.

Defining the Experiment

Step 1) Enter a title for the experiment.

Step 2) Optional, enter a description describing the intent of the experiment.

Step 3) Optional, enter an informed consent text. This is not necessary when you are creating experiments for yourself, but very important in letting other participants know how you will treat their data.

All of the above fields are displayed on the phone when participants are looking for experiments in the “Find Experiments” view and on the server when participants or administrators are looking for the data in their experiments or wanting to modify the experiments.

Add Other Administrators

Optionally, expand the “administrators” view by clicking on it and add other administrators’ email address in a comma-separated list, e.g., jim@gmail.com, betty@gmail.com

Define the Signalling Schedule

Set the scheduling options for how Paco will signal the participant. You can choose from “Self Report” where the user responds manually without any signalling, fixed scheduled signals, e.g., Daily, Weekday, Weekly, Monthly, which schedule at set times, or “ESM (Random)” which randomly samples the user a certain number of times per period between certain start and end hours. While users cannot change the type of schedule, they can customize the parameters to accommodate normal variation in individuals’ schedule. You can prevent them from changing the schedule, but this will likely cause more problems than benefits.

 

Define the Input Items (Questions)

Enter Input items. Be sure to fill out the name field as this is used to show the data in charts and to reference the answer in subsequent conditionally visible questions (this is also known as skip logic in many survey tools).

To create an additional input item after the current input click the ‘+’ button to the far right of the current input item. ‘-’ will delete the current input item.

Input item types include open text questions, list choices, likert scales, numbers, photos and location.

Likert Scales

In Likert scales you can specify the number of steps. We recommend no more than 5 due to the small screens of most phones. If you want 7, use a list.

You can also optionally put labels for the left and right ends of the scale. Keep in mind the screen size limitations when choosing your labels.

Tip: Two-step Likerts are one way to create Yes/No questions.

List Choices

You can also choose a list of options for the user to respond to. They can choice a single item, or you can make it a multiple selection list for them to choose multiple items.

Location

The location input item will try to sample the location services on the phone. This is either the cellular tower and wifi access points nearby, or the gps. The user should be instructed not to use GPS indoors as it will only timeout without providing an answer in almost all situations.

Camera

This input prompts the user to take a photo which will become one of the responses for the given signal. This opens the camera app on the phone and then the user presses the back button when they are done to return to the experimental input form.

Open Text

If you use opentext and would like to make easier long responses, let users know that they can always choose the microphone from the keyboard and speak their answer if it is an appropriate place to do so. This does require internet access to process the speech into text.

Conditionally Visible Questions

Some questions might only be shown based on the response to previous questions. You can mark these by checking the “conditional” checkbox. This will open a panel where you can add an expression that must evaluate to true in order for the question to be shown to the participant.

The expressions are comparisons against the response for a previous question. For example, if you have a question named ‘question1’ and it has a Likert response type with 5 steps  (it could also be a list or a number type), you can write an expression to check the value is either equal (‘==’), less than (‘<’), less than or equal (‘<=’), greater (‘>’), greater than or equal (‘>=’), or not equal (‘!=’), like so,

   

     question1 > 1

Further, you can combine multiple expression with and (‘&&’) and or (‘||’). Here is an example that makes sure question1’s answer is greater than 1 and less than 4.

    question1 > 1 && question1 < 4.

When a participant is responding to inputs in the experiment, each response causes the conditionals to be checked again. If a conditional evaluates to true, the input will be shown. If it evaluates to false, the question will not be shown.

When you are testing against the answer for Likert items the left-most answer will be 1 and it increments as you move right.

When you are testing against the answer for a List item, the first item will be 1 and each item after increments the value.

Publishing Your Experiment

Optionally publish your experiment to other users, or to all users. This is not necessary to share the experiment with your own phone, or with the other administrators you added.

Custom Feedback

This is a way to replace the current feedback page with a custom HTML page. It is very undocumented at this point and very powerful. Please contact Bob (bob@pacoapp.com) to learn more if you want to use it before the documentation is done.

Save Your Experiment

Finally, click ‘Save’, or ‘Update’ if you are editing an existing experiment. This will save your experiment. When it is done you will get a popup that says “Success” or “Failure”. Unfortunately, at the moment, error checking and error messages are very poor. Please email the user group (paco-users@googlegroups.com), or Bob (bob@pacoapp.com) to get help with failures.

Editing Your Experiment

Click on the ‘Administer Experiments’ in the menu above.

Look for the Experiment Title of your experiment.

Click on the title to see the experiment definition and optionally to make edits to the experiment.

Click ‘Save’ to keep your modifications.

Participants will receive an updated experiment within 24 hours if they are within range of a network. You can also have them refresh the experiment definition on demand.

Paco also has a way to inspect the previous answers for a given experiment, at this point called ‘Explore Data’ in the menu item for that experiment. This is the same view you see by default after responding to an experiment.

VII: Looking at Experiment Data on the Server

As you participate in experiments, or as you conduct experiments, you can also examine data and statistics about the experiment on the server. You can see just responses for your own participation or you can see responses for all participants for experiments you are administering.

Examining Responses for an Experiment in which You are Participating

If you want to see only the data for an experiment in which you are participating,

click on ‘Joined Experiments’ in the menu above (if it is not already showing).

Look for the title of the experiment you want to examine.

Choose ‘Charts’ underneath the experiment to see your responses.

Choose ‘Stats’ to see meta-statistics about the response rate.

NOTE: While there are controls on the page for Joined Experiments, you cannot edit the experiment definition here. See that there is no Save button. This is a UI bug that will be remedied in the future.

Examining Responses for All Participants in an Experiment You are Administering

Click on ‘Administer Experiments’ in the menu above.

Look for the title of your experiment. There will be a row of buttons underneath the title.

Choose ‘Stats’ to see meta-statistics about the response rate.

Choose ‘Charts’ to see data for individual items.

Choose ‘Hide’ to make this experiment invisible to everyone but the administrators.

  This is a toggle, that you can later ‘Unhide’ the experiment.

VIII: Exporting Data from the Server

To perform more advanced analysis and visualization of the data from your experiments, or to create mashups, Paco provides two methods to export data from the server.

Exporting Data as CSV

You can export the data in your experiment by clicking on the ‘CSV’ button underneath the experiment title in the list of experiments. This will create a comma-separated-value text file where each row is one response. This file format is supported for import by most statistical tools.

Either for your own data, or for all data (if you are an administrator). Click on ‘Joined Experiments’ or ‘Administer Experiments’ respectively, in the menu above.

Find the title of the experiment you want to export.

Click the ‘CSV’ button underneath the title.

This will download a csv file of the data to your downloads place on your disk.
